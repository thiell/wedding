There is so much to see and do in Southwest France! Below are some of our top recommendations. For a more complete list, try Lonely Planet.

Dune du Pyla

The largest sand dune in Europe and one of the top places to visit in the region. Views of Arcachon Bay from the top are spectacular, and children enjoy playing in the abundance of sand. Visiting at dawn or dusk is particularly recommended.
More from Lonely Planet.

Cap Ferret

An extremely scenic strip of land bordered by Arcachon Bay and the Atlantic Ocean. There are, long strips of sandy beaches, iconic regional architecture, and the reason most people visit - plenty of oyster farms with their affiliated charming and unassuming oyster huts. For ease of exploration, driving around the Bay of Arcachon to get there is recommended, although it can also be reached by frequent ferry from Arcachon.
Older but still largely accurate articles from Condé Nast Traveler and The New York Times.

Saint-Émilion

Saint-Émilion is a charming medieval village located in the heart of the famous Bordeaux wine area. It is a UNESCO-recognized area where world-class wineries, beautiful architecture, and great restaurants can all be found in one small, picturesque town. Bordeaux is a convenient base for a day trip to Saint-Émilion. You can drive on your own or join a tour. If there are any wineries you particularly want to visit, check to see if you need to reserve in advance.
More from Lonely Planet.

The Périgord / Dordogne

An author of a National Geographic Travel article about the area raved, “I am hopelessly, crazily in love with everything about it: the prehistoric caves, the fairy-tale castles, and the resilient locals, who still call the region Périgord.” We also like its foodie accolades – the region is known for its truffles and duck and has an abundance of both local artisan farms and Michelin-starred restaurants. The drive is about 3.5 hours from Arcachon and 2.5 hours from Bordeaux.
The full National Geographic article.

The French Pyrénées

With epic views, an antique train, wildlife, an abundance of hikes, and even the opportunity to sled in the summer months (something called “mountain luge”), it’s no surprise the mountain range that separates France from Spain is often called a playground for lovers of the outdoors. Plus there are regional specialties to try and lots of prehistoric and medieval sites.
More from Lonely Planet article + their top things to do.

Pays Basque

Rick Steves does not include much of Southwest France in his travel guides (which we do not agree with at all). He does include the Pays Basque, though: “The Basque Country is famous for its beaches, culinary scene, and scintillating modern architecture…and for its feisty, industrious natives. It's also simply beautiful: Bright white chalet-style homes with deep-red and green shutters scatter across lush, rolling hills; the Pyrenees Mountains soar high above the Atlantic; and surfers and sardines share the waves.”
More from Rick Steves • More from Lonely Planet • A 2015 article in The New York Times

Northern Spain

Not technically in Southwest France, but we would be remiss if we didn’t mention that San Sebastian is only a 2.5-hour drive away.
